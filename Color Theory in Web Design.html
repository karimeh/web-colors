🎨 Color Theory in Web Design

A quick, practical reference – how colors work together on the screen

🌈 The Color Wheel

Primary: Red, Yellow, Blue  |  Secondary: Green, Orange, Purple  |  Tertiary: mixes like red‑orange, blue‑green, etc.

The wheel helps you choose colors that look good together. Most web palettes start from here.

🔗 Color Harmonies

These formulas guarantee balanced combinations. Use them as a starting point for your website.

Monochromatic
One hue in varying lightness. Clean & simple.
Analogous
Colors side‑by‑side on the wheel. Harmonious & serene.
Complementary
Two opposite colors. High contrast – use sparingly.
Triadic
Three equally spaced hues. Vibrant & balanced.
Tetradic (Double)
Two complementary pairs. Rich but hard to balance.

🧠 Color Psychology (Quick Reference)

Colors evoke emotions. Choose them based on the feeling you want to convey.

Blue – Trust, calm, corporate
Red – Urgency, passion, excitement
Green – Nature, growth, health
Yellow – Optimism, warmth, caution
Orange – Energy, friendly, affordable
Purple – Luxury, creativity, wisdom
Black – Elegance, power, modernity
White – Purity, simplicity, space

💡 Practical Tips for Web

  • 1 60‑30‑10 Rule: 60% dominant color, 30% secondary, 10% accent. Keeps balance.
  • 2 Accessibility first: Ensure text has at least 4.5:1 contrast ratio against background (use tools like WebAIM).
  • 3 Neutrals are your friend: Grays, whites, and blacks give the eye a rest and highlight key elements.
  • 4 Use HSL for flexibility: Define a base hue and adjust lightness/saturation for variations – easier than HEX tweaking.
  • 5 Limit your palette: 3–5 colors (plus neutrals) are usually enough. Too many colors confuse users.
  • 6 Test on real screens: Colors look different on various devices. Always preview on phone and desktop.

🎨 Use these principles as a starting point – great design comes from experimentation.
